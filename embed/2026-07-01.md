### Birthright Citizenship

Like [most countries in the Western 
hemisphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jus_soli), the United States
has *birthright citizenship*, which means anyone born on US soil (with
the very narrow exception of a child of a diplomat) automatically
becomes a US citizen.  

This right was enshrined in the 14^th amendment of the US constitution.

The supreme court has now twice confirmed that, if you’re born on US soil,
you’re a US citizen.  They affirmed it back in 
[1898](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_v._Wong_Kim_Ark), and
they 
[affirmed](https://archive.ph/20260630152601/https://www.nytimes.com/live/2026/06/30/us/birthright-citizenship-supreme-court)
it again [this 
week](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trump_v._Barbara).

The first time I saw someone oppose the 14^th amendment and birthright
citizenship online, long before the outrage and cancel culture of the
2010s, the people on the site were disgusting people, calling Mexican
people “lice” and otherwise being blatantly racist.

### RAM prices: Through the roof

Something is finally being done about RAM prices going through the roof;
a [class action
lawsuit](https://archive.ph/20260629184234/https://kotaku.com/class-action-lawsuit-accuses-the-three-largest-ram-manufacturers-of-colluding-to-drive-up-prices-2000711373) 
has been files accusing the RAM manufactors of working together to
price gouge customers.

When I had to buy a laptop this year because the previous one had
a hardware issue, while I normally get a Core i7 with the maximum
amount of possible memory, this time I instead got a Core i5 with 32gb
of memory—the same amount of memory the computers I bought back in
2017 had.  I can’t wait for memory (and SSD) prices to get sane again;
if they do, I could very well replace my i5 Thinkpad T14s with a newer
T14s with the maximum possible memory and biggest SSD.
