The Grand Prix

For years I have found the Sicilian Defense annoying. If someone played the Sicilian as Black against me, they almost always won, and found the defense intimidating to deal with. It has only been this year when I finally found a way to deal with the Sicilian: The Grand Prix.

For years I tried to understand the Grand Prix, but it was not until I got Morat’s excellent book My First Chess Opening Repertoire (for White) and it has a chapter which explains the Grand Prix in a very easy to digest form.

I will not copy the contents of his book, but the summary is this: White plays 1. e5, Black responds 1. c5, then White plays, in this order 2. Nc3, 3. f4, and 4. Nf3. For the king side bishop, White will play one of Bc4, Bb5, or sometimes Be2. White castles kingside. d3 is usually played.

That’s nice in theory. Now, to show the Grand Prix, I could use one of the games in Morat’s book, but I think it makes more sense to show a game not in that book but still playing a textbook Grand Prix, ideally one played by an elite gransmaster where he goes on to win the game.

Today, Praggnanandhaa (“Pragg”) played the Grand Prix as White in the candidates and won the game. It was a textbook Grand Prix, in fact.

So let’s look at that game to see how to play the Grand Prix.

With the exception of White’s queen rook pawn (left side pawn) being on a4, this is a textbook position for the Sicilian Grand Prix in the early midgame:

  • The knights are on c3 and f3
  • Pawns are on d3, e4, and f4
  • The kingside (light-squared) bishop is on c4
  • White has castled on the kingside

If Black were to castle on the kingside, the general plan of attack is this:

  • White’s queenside (dark-squared) bishop goes on h6
  • White’s knight is on g5
  • White’s queen is on h4
Since Giri didn’t castle kingside in this game, let’s look at the game as played:
